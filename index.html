Slide 1

Fundamental Concepts

A Network Operating System coordinates users, devices, and services over a network. It extends core operating system responsibilities beyond one machine.

Resource Sharing

Provides controlled access to files, printers, storage, and applications across multiple computers.

User and Security Management

Authenticates users, enforces permissions, and protects shared services from unauthorized access.

Communication Services

Supports protocols and service discovery so systems can exchange data reliably across the network.

Centralized Administration

Helps administrators monitor nodes, configure policies, and maintain service availability from a common point.

Slide 2

How a NOS Operates

The system receives requests, checks identity and policy, selects a service path, and delivers shared resources while balancing fairness and efficiency.

01

Connect

A device joins the network and discovers available services.

02

Authenticate

The NOS validates identity through credentials or directory services.

03

Authorize

Policies determine which files, devices, or applications the user can access.

04

Schedule

Requests are ordered so shared bandwidth and server time are used effectively.

05

Deliver

Data moves through the network while the NOS tracks reliability and performance.

Slide 3

Why Queueing and Scheduling Matter

When many packets or requests arrive at once, a NOS must decide who waits, who goes first, and how to avoid starvation or congestion.

Queueing

Queueing describes how incoming packets line up when demand exceeds immediate service capacity.

Scheduling

Scheduling chooses the next packet or flow to process based on a policy such as fairness, priority, or simplicity.

Real Tradeoffs

Fast service for critical traffic can reduce delay, but it can also starve lower-priority traffic if the policy is poorly designed.
